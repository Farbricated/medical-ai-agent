Cardiovascular Disease - Cardiology Reference

Acute Myocardial Infarction (Heart Attack):
AMI occurs when blood flow to a part of the heart is blocked, causing myocardial necrosis. Classified as STEMI (ST-elevation MI) or NSTEMI.

Symptoms: Chest pain or pressure radiating to left arm, jaw, or back. Diaphoresis, nausea, dyspnea, and lightheadedness. Women may present atypically with fatigue and epigastric pain.

Diagnosis: ECG changes (ST elevation in STEMI), elevated troponin I and T, CK-MB. Echocardiography to assess wall motion abnormalities.

Treatment: Immediate reperfusion therapy (PCI preferred within 90 minutes for STEMI). Antiplatelet therapy: aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor (clopidogrel, ticagrelor). Anticoagulation, beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors, statins.

Hypertension:
Blood pressure >= 140/90 mmHg is hypertension. >= 180/120 is hypertensive crisis.
Primary (essential) hypertension accounts for 95% of cases.
First-line medications: ACE inhibitors, ARBs, calcium channel blockers, thiazide diuretics.
Target BP < 130/80 mmHg for most patients.

Heart Failure:
HFrEF (reduced ejection fraction, EF < 40%) vs HFpEF (preserved EF >= 50%).
Symptoms: dyspnea on exertion, orthopnea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, peripheral edema.
Treatment for HFrEF: ACE inhibitors/ARBs/ARNIs, beta-blockers, mineralocorticoid antagonists, SGLT2 inhibitors, diuretics.

Atrial Fibrillation:
Most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia. Risk of stroke requires anticoagulation (CHA2DS2-VASc score).
Rate control: beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers, digoxin.
Rhythm control: antiarrhythmics (amiodarone, flecainide), cardioversion, ablation.

Chest Pain Evaluation:
Differential includes ACS, aortic dissection, pulmonary embolism, pericarditis, costochondritis, GERD.
Always rule out life-threatening causes first with ECG, troponin, chest X-ray.
